## What Are the Main Cost Categories for a Texas Hotel Build?

Every hotel project budget breaks into four buckets: hard costs, soft costs, FF&E/OS&E, and financing/pre-opening.

### Hard Costs

Hard costs cover site work, foundation, structural, MEP (mechanical, electrical, plumbing), and finishes. In Texas, concrete and structural steel costs remain elevated from 2024–2025 tariff cycles, adding 6–9% to structural packages compared to pre-2024 baselines on recent Maxx Builders projects.

### Soft Costs

Soft costs typically run 20–30% of hard costs and include:
- Architecture and engineering (typically 8–12% of hard costs)
- Permits and impact fees (varies widely by municipality — Austin-area municipalities routinely add $500K–$2M in fees for select-service projects above 100 keys)
- Pre-construction and project management
- Legal, title, and financing fees

### FF&E and OS&E

Furniture, Fixtures & Equipment plus Operating Supplies & Equipment add $8,000–$35,000 per key depending on brand and tier. Brand flags (Marriott, Hilton, IHG) impose specific FF&E standards with approved vendor lists that owners must budget against the brand's product improvement plan timeline.

### Financing and Pre-Opening

Interest carry during construction, pre-opening staffing, and working capital typically add another 5–10% to total project cost for a standard 24-month build.

## What Drives Hotel Construction Cost Overruns in Texas?

The three most common causes of cost overruns on Texas hotel projects are scope creep, permitting delays, and subcontractor availability gaps.

**Scope creep** is the leading driver. Late changes to room count, brand upgrade requirements, or owner program additions after GMP execution are costly. Lock scope before finalizing the GMP.

**Permitting delays** in high-growth Texas markets (Austin, Round Rock, Kyle) routinely add 3–6 months to project schedules, compounding interest carry.

**Subcontractor depth** is thin in secondary markets. Specialty trades — commercial kitchen, elevator, curtain wall — may require importing crews, which inflates both cost and schedule risk.

## How to Estimate Your Texas Hotel Construction Budget: A Step-by-Step Process

Getting to a reliable budget number before committing to land or a franchise agreement requires working through five sequential steps.

**Step 1: Define the program.** Establish the key count, gross SF per key (typically 400–600 SF/key for select-service), and the full amenity set including pool, fitness center, and any food and beverage. Gross SF drives everything downstream — do not skip this.

**Step 2: Conceptual estimate.** Apply the $/SF ranges from this guide to your total gross SF. Add 25–30% for soft costs and FF&E. This produces a rough order of magnitude with a ±30% band — sufficient for go/no-go on land control, not for financing.

**Step 3: Select delivery method and engage a GC for pre-construction services.** A qualified GC can provide a ±15% cost estimate from schematic drawings. Pre-construction fees are typically $25,000–$75,000 for a select-service project and are well spent — the alternative is a surprise at bid day.

**Step 4: Schematic design estimate.** At approximately 30% design completion, the GC and design team reconcile the estimate against the owner's budget. This is the last practical point for scope adjustments without cost penalty — changes after this milestone carry progressively higher redesign and re-permitting costs.

**Step 5: GMP.** At 90–100% construction documents, the GC locks a Guaranteed Maximum Price. Contingency shifts from the owner's budget to the GC's risk. Savings below the GMP are typically shared per the contract terms.

**Worked example:** 120-key select-service in DFW, 58,000 gross SF at $260/SF hard cost = $15.1M hard cost. Add $4.5M for soft costs and FF&E = $19.6M total project cost before land.

## Texas Hotel Construction Financing: What Lenders Require in 2026

Securing construction financing is the critical path item for most Texas hotel projects. Understanding lender requirements before committing to a franchise agreement saves significant time and cost.

**Construction-to-permanent loan:** Standard structure for select-service projects. Expect 65–70% loan-to-cost, an 18–24 month construction period, and interest-only payments during the build. Regional Texas banks including Frost Bank, Prosperity Bank, and Cadence Bank are active in hotel construction lending for select-service projects with qualified operators.

**SBA 504:** Best fit for owner-operated properties under $20M total project cost. The structure is 10% owner equity, 40% SBA debenture at below-market fixed rate (set at debenture sale), and 50% bank conventional loan. Current SBA 504 CDC tranche fixed rates are approximately 5.5–6.5% in mid-2026, making this the lowest-cost permanent capital available for qualifying projects.

**Current construction loan rates (mid-2026):** Conventional construction loans in Texas are pricing at prime plus 1.5–2.5% for qualified borrowers with executed franchise agreements and a track record.

**Lender documentation package — minimum requirements:**

- Executed franchise agreement or letter of intent from brand
- Market feasibility study from a qualified hotel consultant
- Pre-qualification of GC with bonding capacity and hotel project references
- AIA GMP contract or letter of intent
- Title and survey
- Environmental Phase I (Phase II if flagged by Phase I)

**Interest reserve sizing:** Budget 6–8% of hard cost to cover interest carry during the construction period. Underfunding this line is one of the most common mistakes on first-time hotel developments — it creates a liquidity squeeze in the final months before certificate of occupancy.

| Loan Type | LTV/LTC | Term | Best For |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Construction-to-Perm (conventional) | 65–70% LTC | 18–24 mo construction + 20–25 yr perm | Experienced operators, larger projects |
| SBA 504 | 90% LTC (10% equity) | 20–25 yr debenture | Owner-operated, under $20M, first-time developers |
| Bridge / Mezzanine | 75–80% LTC | 12–24 mo | Value-add, repositioning, gap fill |

## Texas Hotel Construction Timeline: From Land Control to Opening Day

A realistic schedule is the single most underestimated input in hotel pro formas. The table below shows phase-by-phase durations with Texas-specific permit timelines.

| Phase | Duration | Texas Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Land control / feasibility | 1–2 months | LOI, due diligence, franchise inquiry |
| Franchise application and approval | 3–6 months | Run in parallel with design — do not wait |
| Schematic design | 2–3 months | Program lock; GC enters for pre-construction |
| Design development + construction documents | 3–5 months | 90–100% CDs required for GMP and permit |
| TDLR accessibility review + building permit | 2–5 months | Austin: 5–9 mo; DFW: 2–4 mo; Houston: 3–5 mo; San Antonio: 2–3 mo |
| Bidding / GMP finalization | 4–6 weeks | Long-lead procurement begins immediately at GMP |
| Construction | 14–20 months | 4-story wood-frame select-service; steel adds 2–4 months |
| Pre-opening + brand inspection | 2–3 months | Staffing, soft goods install, brand punch walk |
| **Total: land control to opening** | **28–42 months** | Secondary markets at low end; Austin at high end |

Franchise application runs in parallel with early design — this is the most commonly misunderstood scheduling issue on first hotel projects. Waiting to apply until design is complete adds 3–6 months to the critical path with zero offsetting benefit.

Long-lead procurement — structural steel, elevators, commercial kitchen equipment, and brand-specified FF&E — should be locked under purchase order at or before permit issuance. In Texas secondary markets with limited subcontractor depth, early procurement is the single most effective schedule risk mitigation available to owners.

## Case Study: 120-Key Select-Service Hotel, Texas Hill Country Market

A physician group and regional developer engaged Maxx Builders for a 120-key select-service hotel project outside San Antonio in a growing Hill Country gateway market. The owner group had real estate experience but was building their first hospitality asset.

The project used a GMP delivery structure, with Maxx providing pre-construction services beginning at the schematic design phase. The pre-construction engagement surfaced two budget risks early: the site's rock profile added approximately $280,000 to the foundation scope (not reflected in the conceptual estimate), and the brand's current FF&E standards had been updated since the owner's pro forma was built, adding $1,100 per key to the FF&E line.

Both items were identified and reconciled before permit issuance, preventing mid-construction change orders.

Early procurement of the structural steel package — locked at permit issuance, 11 weeks before the trade would have been bid conventionally — and brand-mandated FF&E ordered at framing completion kept the project on a 16-month construction schedule despite tight labor availability in that market. The Hill Country market has limited commercial subcontractor depth; importing specialty trades (elevator, curtain wall glazing, commercial kitchen) was anticipated in the GMP and scheduled into the sequence rather than bid out at the last minute.

Total hard cost came in within 2% of the original GMP. The project reached certificate of occupancy on schedule and achieved brand approval on the first inspection walk.

**Key lesson:** In Texas secondary markets, the combination of early GC engagement, pre-construction cost validation, and locked long-lead procurement is not a luxury — it is the difference between a hotel that opens on schedule and one that does not.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to build a 100-room hotel in Texas?

A 100-room select-service hotel in Texas in 2026 costs approximately $18M–$28M in total project cost, assuming roughly 55,000–65,000 SF of gross floor area, current Texas labor rates, and a standard midscale flag. Urban markets (Austin, DFW) will skew toward the top of that range; secondary markets toward the lower end. These figures are based on Maxx Builders project experience and current market conditions.

### How long does hotel construction take in Texas?

Most hotel projects in Texas take 18–30 months from permit issuance to certificate of occupancy. Full-service and luxury projects in urban markets can run 30–42 months when permitting time is included. Adding pre-design and entitlement, total development timelines of 36–48 months are common for projects in Austin and DFW.

### What brand flags are most cost-efficient in Texas?

Select-service flags from Marriott (Fairfield, Courtyard), Hilton (Hampton Inn, Tru), and IHG (Holiday Inn Express) are the most cost-efficient for Texas markets in 2026. Tru by Hilton's compact prototype delivers the lowest cost per key of the major flags. Soft brands and independent boutiques can save 5–10% in FF&E by avoiding brand-mandated vendors but require stronger management infrastructure and carry higher lender scrutiny.

### What is the difference between hard cost and total project cost?

Hard cost is the construction contract value — labor and materials for the physical structure. Total project cost adds soft costs, FF&E/OS&E, land, financing carry, and pre-opening expenses. For most Texas hotel projects, total project cost runs 1.35–1.55x the hard cost.
