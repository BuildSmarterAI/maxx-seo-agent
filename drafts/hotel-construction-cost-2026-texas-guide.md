## What Does It Cost to Build a Hotel in Texas in 2026?

Building a hotel in Texas in 2026 costs between $245,000 and $995,000+ per key in total development cost, depending on hotel type, location, and amenity program. A standard 100-key select-service project runs approximately $40–$45 million. These figures are higher than pre-2020 baselines because of persistent MEP labor shortages, above-trend material pricing, and brand prototype upgrades that have become standard across every major flag.

This guide uses data from completed Maxx Builders deliveries — including a Home2Suites by Hilton in Richmond, a Comfort Suites in Pasadena, and a 114,700 sq ft Holiday Inn Express in Pflugerville — alongside third-party benchmarks from RSMeans and Gordian. ## Section 1 — Cost Metrics: Per Key vs. Per Square Foot

### What Is Cost Per Key and Why Does It Still Lead Feasibility?

Cost per key is the primary underwriting metric in 2026 hospitality finance. It lets lenders, investors, and REITs compare hotels across markets and brand families without adjusting for varying room sizes. It connects directly to ADR, RevPAR, and NOI, which is why it anchors almost every lender conversation.

Use cost per key for: feasibility studies, land offers, and initial lender underwriting.

### When Should You Use Cost Per Square Foot Instead?

Cost per SF aligns with CSI divisions and subcontractor pricing. When a building system changes — HVAC sizing, structural system, envelope specification — the cost per SF reflects the impact first. It is the right tool for design-phase decisions, value engineering, and estimating.

Use cost per SF for: design development, value engineering, and subcontractor scope comparison.

### Why Is Texas Hotel Construction Cost Elevated in 2026?

Several Texas-specific conditions compound national trends:

- **High humidity** requires larger, more expensive HVAC systems.
- **Coastal wind ratings** (Gulf Coast markets) demand heavier exterior envelope systems.
- **Drainage and detention rules** increase civil and sitework costs across most metros.
- **Industrial and logistics growth** competes for MEP trades, keeping labor rates high.
- **Structured parking** requirements in urban infill sites add substantial concrete and steel cost.

## Section 4 — CSI Divisions That Drive Hotel Construction Cost

Controlling hotel construction cost means understanding where the money actually goes. The divisions below account for the largest share of hard cost variation between hotel types.

### Division 3 — Concrete

Foundations, podium slabs, grade beams, and flatwork. In Texas, expansive soils and parking podium requirements make this a primary cost driver on urban and suburban sites alike.

### Division 5 — Metals

Structural steel for long spans, rooftop mechanical support, ballroom roofs, and canopies. Full-service and dual-brand hotels carry more steel than select-service prototypes.

### Division 7 — Thermal and Moisture Protection

Roofing, insulation, air barriers, and waterproofing. Texas heat and Gulf Coast storm exposure put more demand on this division than comparable projects in cooler, drier climates.

### Division 8 — Openings

Storefronts, windows, and doors. Coastal and near-coastal markets require impact-rated glazing, which adds to material and installation cost.

### Division 9 — Finishes

Guest room finishes, corridors, lobbies, and public spaces. Brand prototype refreshes have moved baseline finish expectations upward across most flags over the past two years.

### Divisions 11–14 — Equipment and Systems

Elevators, foodservice equipment, commercial laundry, fitness equipment, and other operating systems. These divisions explain the cost spread between a limited-service flag and a full-service hotel more than any single finish choice.

## Section 5 — Metro-by-Metro Texas Hotel Construction Cost Differences

### How Does Houston Compare to the Texas Baseline?

Houston runs 5–8% above the DFW baseline in 2026. The premium comes from oversized HVAC loads driven by humidity, floodplain and detention compliance costs on most sites, and coastal wind envelope requirements in Gulf-adjacent submarkets.

### Why Is Austin the Most Expensive Texas Market for Hotel Construction?

Austin is 8–12% above the DFW baseline — the highest premium in the state. The combination of the tightest MEP labor market in Texas, lifestyle and boutique hotel programs that push finish and amenity levels above brand minimums, and complex entitlement environments in central markets all contribute.

### What Makes Dallas–Fort Worth the Baseline Market?

DFW benefits from the largest subcontractor pool in Texas and the highest bid density across all trades. It is the market where national cost guides and local experience most often align, making it a natural reference point for statewide benchmarking.

### Is San Antonio Really Cheaper to Build In?

San Antonio typically runs about 5% below the DFW baseline. Lower trade and labor rates, less complex sitework in many submarkets, and a more stable entitlement environment all contribute. Strong tourism demand supports the hospitality pipeline, but construction cost remains lower than the major metros.

## Section 7 — Market Trends Shaping Hotel Construction Cost in 2026

Several forces are keeping Texas hotel construction cost elevated even as broader inflation moderates:

- **MEP labor constraints**: Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing subcontractors remain fully committed across Texas. Lead time and pricing risk in these trades are the top schedule and cost threats on most hotel projects.
- **HVAC and electrical equipment inflation**: Higher performance standards and larger system loads compound raw equipment price increases.
- **FF&E pricing at a higher plateau**: Lead times have normalized since 2022, but prices have not returned to pre-2020 levels. Budget at least 10–15% above 2019 FF&E benchmarks.
- **Steel and concrete volatility**: Full-service and luxury structural packages retain more pricing uncertainty than select-service or extended-stay products.
- **ESG and energy mandates**: EV charging infrastructure, improved envelope performance, and more efficient mechanical systems all increase initial capital cost.
- **Brand prototype refresh cycles**: Every major flag has rolled out updated lobby, room, and amenity standards since 2022. The new baseline costs more to build than the 2018 prototype.

A project that underwrote at a 2018 cost model may not be feasible at 2026 actual cost. Refresh all assumptions before submitting to a lender.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the average hotel construction cost per key in Texas in 2026?

Limited-service hotels average approximately $245,000 per key in total development cost. Luxury hotels exceed $995,000 per key. Select-service — the most common new-build segment in Texas — averages around $421,000 per key.

### How much does it cost to build a 100-room hotel in Texas in 2026?

A 100-key select-service hotel typically runs $40–$45 million in 2026, including hard costs, soft costs, and FF&E. The exact number depends on the specific metro, brand prototype, amenity program, and site conditions.

### Which amenities add the most to hotel construction cost?

Indoor pools, structured parking, full-service kitchens, ballrooms, and rooftop spaces carry the highest per-unit cost impact. Structured parking in particular can add $22,000–$38,000 per space, which compounds quickly on mid-rise urban projects.

### Why are hotel construction costs higher in Texas than in some other U.S. markets?

Climate-driven HVAC oversizing, coastal envelope requirements, drainage and detention compliance costs, industrial competition for MEP trades, and frequent structured parking requirements all push Texas costs above the national average in most hotel segments.

### Can value engineering still reduce hotel construction cost in 2026?

Yes — if it targets the right scopes. VE applied early to structure, envelope, MEP systems, and parking can reduce total hard cost by 3–8% without degrading the guest experience or violating brand standards. VE applied to finishes late in design rarely moves the budget meaningfully.

### What is the difference between hard cost and soft cost in hotel development?

Hard cost covers physical construction: site work, structure, MEP systems, finishes, FF&E, and OS&E. Soft cost covers design, engineering, permitting, financing fees, pre-opening expenses, legal, and owner overhead. For a Texas select-service hotel in 2026, hard cost is roughly 60–65% of total development cost and soft cost is roughly 20–22%.
