# Fort Worth Commercial Construction Cost Per Square Foot (2026)

*By Harris Khan, Co-Founder, Maxx Builders · Last updated June 25, 2026*

**Commercial construction in Fort Worth costs roughly $140–$450 per square foot in 2026**, depending on building type, finish level, and site conditions. Tilt-up warehouse and light-industrial shells anchor the low end; restaurants, medical offices, and high-finish retail push toward the top. Because Fort Worth sits inside the Dallas–Fort Worth (DFW) metroplex, its pricing tracks the DFW baseline closely — typically within a few points of Dallas proper, with sub-market swings driven by the Alliance/north Fort Worth industrial corridor.

- **Typical range:** $140–$450/sq ft for new commercial construction in Fort Worth (2026), consistent with the broader DFW market.

## How Does Fort Worth Compare to Dallas and the Rest of DFW?

**Fort Worth and Dallas are effectively one construction market — budget for cost parity, not a Fort Worth discount.** Subcontractors, material suppliers, and skilled trades move freely across the metroplex, so the per-square-foot baseline is shared. What differs is sub-market dynamics, not headline pricing.

| DFW Sub-Market | Cost Posture vs. DFW Baseline | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Dallas (core) | Baseline | Deepest subcontractor pool, highest project density |
| Fort Worth (core/downtown) | ≈ Baseline | Same labor pool; tighter urban sites can add cost |
| Alliance / North Fort Worth | Baseline to slightly favorable for industrial | Master-planned industrial corridor, repeatable tilt-up scope |
| Suburban DFW (Arlington, Irving) | ≈ Baseline | Easier sites can trim civil costs |

The **Alliance corridor** in far north Fort Worth is one of the largest master-planned industrial districts in the country. For repeatable tilt-up distribution product there, economies of scale in earthwork and panel work can pull industrial costs toward the low end of the range — one of the few places where Fort Worth may slightly beat the metro average.

## What Drives Fort Worth Commercial Construction Costs?

### Structural System Choice

The frame is the first major cost fork. Tilt-up concrete is the DFW workhorse for warehouse, retail, and many office buildings — fast, durable, and cost-efficient at scale. Pre-engineered metal buildings (PEMBs) are cheaper for simple industrial spans but constrain bay sizing. Structural steel costs more but is required for tall, irregular, or crane-served buildings. The right system is a function of use, not just budget.

### MEP and Finish Level

Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing — plus interior finish — are where building *type* turns into building *cost*. A warehouse needs minimal MEP; a restaurant or clinic needs heavy kitchen exhaust, grease interceptors, medical gas, or specialized HVAC. This is why a restaurant can cost three to four times a warehouse on the same per-SF basis.

### Site and Civil Work

Site work is the most underestimated line in any budget. Grading, detention, utility extensions, and paving can swing total cost by $10–$40/sq ft depending on the parcel. Flat, fully served sites in master-planned districts are cheapest; infill and constrained urban Fort Worth sites cost more.

### 2026 Material and Labor Escalation

Texas remains one of the busiest nonresidential construction markets in the country, which keeps skilled-trade labor tight across DFW. Material pricing — steel, concrete, glazing, switchgear — remains volatile, and long-lead items such as electrical gear and rooftop units continue to carry extended lead times that can drive both cost and schedule.

### Delivery Model

How you contract the work affects total cost as much as what you build. Design-build and CMAR (construction-manager-at-risk) models let the builder price and value-engineer during design, reducing change orders later. See [design-build construction](https://www.maxxbuilders.com/design-build-construction-houston/) for how the model controls cost — the same principles apply across Texas markets including Fort Worth.

## How Long Does Commercial Permitting Take in Fort Worth?

**Plan review and permitting in the City of Fort Worth typically runs from several weeks to a few months for commercial projects, depending on scope, submittal completeness, and current department backlog.** Permitting time is real schedule — and schedule is carrying cost — so it belongs in any honest cost conversation.

The fastest path through permitting is a complete, coordinated submittal — which is a preconstruction discipline, not luck. Front-loading code review and agency coordination during design is the most reliable way to protect the schedule.

## How Do Fort Worth Construction Costs Break Down by Category?

A rule-of-thumb cost breakdown for a mid-spec DFW commercial building:

| Cost Category | % of Total Construction Cost |
|---|---|
| Structural / shell (foundation, frame, roof, walls) | 30–45% |
| Site work and civil / utilities | 10–20% |
| MEP (mechanical, electrical, plumbing) | 15–25% |
| Interior finish / tenant improvement | 10–20% |
| Fire/life-safety (sprinkler, alarm) | 3–6% |
| General conditions, fees, contingency | 10–15% |

The MEP and finish bands shift substantially between a warehouse and a restaurant or clinic — the higher the mechanical and finish demand, the larger those two categories grow as a share of total cost.

## How Can You Control Commercial Construction Costs in Fort Worth?

**The cheapest savings happen before a shovel hits the ground.** Value engineering during design — not during construction — is the most effective cost-control lever available. Three moves matter most:

1. **Engage the builder early (preconstruction).** Real-time pricing during design catches budget-busters while they are still cheap to change on paper.
2. **Choose the right delivery model.** Design-build and CMAR consolidate accountability and reduce the change-order risk that inflates lump-sum jobs.
3. **Carry honest contingency.** Early-stage estimates with incomplete drawings should carry more contingency, not less — under-budgeting guarantees a painful reconciliation later.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to build a commercial building in Fort Worth in 2026?

Commercial construction in Fort Worth costs roughly **$140–$450 per square foot** in 2026, depending on building type and finish. Warehouse and industrial shells sit at the low end; restaurants, medical offices, and Class A office sit at the high end. These figures exclude land, FF&E, and major off-site work.

### Is it cheaper to build in Fort Worth than in Dallas?

Not meaningfully. Fort Worth and Dallas share one metroplex labor force, subcontractor base, and supply chain, so per-square-foot costs are at parity. The biggest cost differences come from the specific site and sub-market — for example, the Alliance industrial corridor favoring repeatable tilt-up product — not from the city line between Fort Worth and Dallas.

### How much does it cost to build a warehouse in Fort Worth?

A warehouse in Fort Worth typically runs **$75–$160 per square foot** for the shell, depending on clear height, dock count, and fire suppression. ### How much does it cost to build an office in Fort Worth?

Ground-up Class A office in Fort Worth generally runs **$200–$400 per square foot**, driven by structure, curtain wall, and common-area finish. Tenant-improvement-only office build-outs in existing shells cost far less per square foot than ground-up construction.

### How long does commercial permitting take in Fort Worth?

City of Fort Worth commercial plan review and permitting typically takes from several weeks to a few months, depending on project scope, submittal completeness, and department workload. A complete, coordinated submittal is the most reliable way to shorten review time.

### What contingency should I budget for a Fort Worth commercial project?

Budget **10–15% contingency** on a project with complete construction documents, and **20–25%** on early-stage estimates with incomplete design. Value engineering during design is the most effective way to protect the budget before construction locks in costs.
