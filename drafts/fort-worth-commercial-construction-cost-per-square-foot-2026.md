# Fort Worth Commercial Construction Cost Per Square Foot (2026)

**Commercial construction in Fort Worth costs roughly $140–$450 per square foot in 2026**, depending on building type, finish level, and site conditions. Tilt-up warehouse and light-industrial shells anchor the low end; restaurants, medical offices, and high-finish retail push toward the top. Because Fort Worth sits inside the Dallas–Fort Worth (DFW) metroplex, its pricing tracks the DFW baseline closely — typically within a few points of Dallas proper, with sub-market swings driven by the Alliance/north Fort Worth industrial corridor.

## How Does Fort Worth Compare to Dallas and the Rest of DFW?

**Fort Worth and Dallas are effectively one construction market — budget for cost parity, not a Fort Worth discount.** Subcontractors, material suppliers, and skilled trades move freely across the metroplex, so the per-square-foot baseline is shared. What differs is sub-market dynamics, not headline pricing.

| DFW Sub-Market | Cost Posture vs. DFW Baseline | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Dallas (core) | Baseline | Deepest subcontractor pool, highest project density |
| Fort Worth (core/downtown) | ≈ Baseline | Same labor pool; tighter urban sites can add cost |
| Alliance / North Fort Worth | Baseline to slightly favorable for industrial | Master-planned industrial corridor, repeatable tilt-up scope |
| Suburban DFW (Arlington, Irving) | ≈ Baseline | Easier sites can trim civil costs |

The **Alliance corridor** in far north Fort Worth is one of the largest master-planned industrial districts in the country. For repeatable tilt-up distribution product there, economies of scale in earthwork and panel work can pull industrial costs toward the low end of the range — one of the few places where Fort Worth may slightly beat the metro average.

## What Drives Fort Worth Commercial Construction Costs?

### Structural System Choice

The frame is the first major cost fork. Tilt-up concrete is the DFW workhorse for warehouse, retail, and many office buildings — fast, durable, and cost-efficient at scale. Pre-engineered metal buildings (PEMBs) are cheaper for simple industrial spans but constrain bay sizing. Structural steel costs more but is required for tall, irregular, or crane-served buildings. The right system is a function of use, not just budget.

### MEP and Finish Level

Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing — plus interior finish — are where building *type* turns into building *cost*. A warehouse needs minimal MEP; a restaurant or clinic needs heavy kitchen exhaust, grease interceptors, medical gas, or specialized HVAC. This is why a restaurant can cost three to four times a warehouse on the same per-SF basis.

### Site and Civil Work

Site work is the most underestimated line in any budget. Grading, detention, utility extensions, and paving can swing total cost by $10–$40/sq ft depending on the parcel. Flat, fully served sites in master-planned districts are cheapest; infill and constrained urban Fort Worth sites cost more.

### 2026 Material and Labor Escalation

Texas remains one of the busiest nonresidential construction markets in the country, which keeps skilled-trade labor tight across DFW. ### Delivery Model

How you contract the work affects total cost as much as what you build. Design-build and CMAR (construction-manager-at-risk) models let the builder price and value-engineer during design, reducing change orders later. ## How Can You Control Commercial Construction Costs in Fort Worth?

**The cheapest savings happen before a shovel hits the ground.** Value engineering during design — not during construction — is the most effective cost-control lever available. Three moves matter most:

1. **Engage the builder early (preconstruction).** Real-time pricing during design catches budget-busters while they are still cheap to change on paper.
2. **Choose the right delivery model.** Design-build and CMAR consolidate accountability and reduce the change-order risk that inflates lump-sum jobs.
3. **Carry honest contingency.** Early-stage estimates with incomplete drawings should carry more contingency, not less — under-budgeting guarantees a painful reconciliation later.
