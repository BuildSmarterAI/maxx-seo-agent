## Hotel Construction: The Short Answer

Building a hotel typically takes 18–36 months from groundbreaking to certificate of occupancy and costs $150,000–$600,000+ per key depending on brand standards, location, and finish level. Success depends on aligning the development pro forma with the construction schedule from day one — cost overruns almost always trace back to scope changes made after permit submission.

## What Does It Cost to Build a Hotel?

The fully loaded cost to build a hotel in the United States ranges from roughly **$150,000 to over $600,000 per key**, with branded limited-service prototypes (Hampton Inn, Fairfield, etc.) clustering between $160,000–$230,000 per key and upper-upscale full-service properties ranging from $400,000–$700,000+ per key.

| Hotel Tier | Typical Keys | Hard Cost Per Key | Soft Cost % of Hard | FF&E Per Key |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Economy / Budget | 60–120 | $100K–$150K | 15–20% | $5K–$10K |
| Limited-Service | 80–150 | $150K–$230K | 18–22% | $10K–$18K |
| Select-Service | 100–200 | $230K–$320K | 20–25% | $20K–$35K |
| Full-Service | 150–500+ | $350K–$600K | 25–35% | $40K–$80K |
| Luxury / Resort | 100–400 | $600K–$1.5M+ | 30–40% | $80K–$200K+ |

Hard costs include sitework, structure, MEP, and finishes. Soft costs include design, permits, FF&E procurement management, pre-opening, and financing carry. **Land is not included.**

### What Drives Cost Overruns?

The three most common drivers of hotel construction cost overruns:

1. **Scope creep during design development.** Brand PIPs (property improvement plans) and flag requirements added after schematic design blow up the GMP. Lock scope before permit submission.
2. **Labor market conditions.** In tight labor markets, subcontractor pricing can shift 8–15% between budget and bid. Build a 10–15% contingency into the owner's pro forma, not just the GC's line.
3. **Utility infrastructure surprises.** Inadequate water/sewer capacity, transformer upgrades, or telecom conduit issues discovered during sitework routinely add $200K–$500K+. Commission a full utility infrastructure study during due diligence.

## How Long Does It Take to Build a Hotel?

A **limited-service prototype hotel (80–150 keys, 4–6 stories)** on a clean, entitled site can be constructed in **12–18 months** from groundbreaking. Add 6–18 months for entitlement and permitting, and the full development timeline from site control to ribbon-cutting is typically **24–36 months**.

### Hotel Construction Timeline Phases

| Phase | Typical Duration | Key Milestones |
|---|---|---|
| Site Control & Due Diligence | 60–120 days | Feasibility, soil reports, utility study |
| Entitlement / Zoning | 3–18 months | CUP, variance, public hearings |
| Design & Brand Approval | 4–8 months | SD, DD, CD, brand prototype sign-off |
| Permitting | 2–6 months | Building, fire, MEP permits |
| Sitework & Foundation | 2–3 months | Excavation, footings, slab on grade |
| Structural Frame | 3–6 months | Concrete/steel/wood-frame per system |
| MEP Rough-In | 3–5 months | Plumbing, HVAC, electrical rough |
| Interior Finishes | 3–5 months | Drywall, flooring, tile, millwork |
| FF&E Installation | 6–8 weeks | Furniture, fixtures, equipment |
| Commissioning & Punch | 4–8 weeks | Life safety, brand walk, CO |

### When to Start FF&E Procurement

Order FF&E — furniture, fixtures, and equipment — no later than **12 months before the target opening date**. Lead times on custom millwork, elevators, and imported tile regularly exceed 20–26 weeks. A late FF&E delivery is the most common cause of a "construction-complete" project sitting dark for 60–90 days before opening.

## Hotel Construction Permits: What You Need and Why It Takes So Long

Every hotel project requires a **building permit** from the local authority having jurisdiction (AHJ), plus separate permits for mechanical, electrical, plumbing, and fire suppression systems. High-rise or mixed-use projects trigger additional state-level reviews.

### Permit Categories for Hotel Construction

- **Building permit** — structural, architectural, life safety
- **MEP permits** — mechanical, electrical, plumbing (often sub-permits under the main building permit)
- **Fire suppression** — separate review by fire marshal in most jurisdictions
- **Health department** — required for any food and beverage operation, pool, or spa
- **Signage** — separate permit, often with a design review board
- **Encroachment / access** — required if the project modifies a public right of way

**Tip:** Submit for permits while the construction documents are still in final review — a 2–3 week overlap between the final CD review and permit submission can compress the overall schedule by 6–8 weeks.

## Choosing a Hotel General Contractor

Not every commercial GC is equipped for hospitality construction. Hotels combine the schedule pressure of retail (a soft opening date with a PR campaign attached) with the systems complexity of healthcare (life safety, BAS, multiple utility services) and the finish tolerance of high-end residential.

### What to Evaluate in a Hotel GC

- **Hospitality-specific portfolio:** How many keys delivered? What flag families? Brand relationships matter — a GC who has built Hampton Inns before knows where Hilton's prototype QA team will push back.
- **Pre-construction capability:** Can they provide a GMP with sufficient trade-level detail to hold? Pre-construction services that shortcut to a lump-sum bid without subcontractor buy-in are a red flag.
- **Self-perform scope:** GCs who self-perform concrete, framing, or finishes have tighter schedule control in those scopes — critical on a hospitality project where the schedule is non-negotiable.
- **Brand-approved subcontractor network:** Marriott, Hilton, and IHG maintain approved vendor lists for FF&E installation, PTACs, and sometimes MEP work. Confirm your GC's subs can qualify.
- **Insurance and bonding:** Verify that the GC can bond the full contract value and carries the minimum COI limits required by your lender and flag.

## Hotel Construction Process: Phase-by-Phase Walkthrough

### Phase 1 — Pre-Construction

Pre-construction is where the budget is won or lost. A thorough pre-con process includes:

- **Site analysis:** Geotechnical report, ALTA survey, phase I/II environmental, utility infrastructure study.
- **Design coordination:** Architect, MEP engineers, civil, landscape, and brand prototype review all running in parallel.
- **Estimating:** Open-book estimating with real subcontractor pricing at each design milestone (SD, DD, final CD).
- **Schedule development:** Master CPM schedule built with the trade partners who will actually execute it.

### Phase 2 — Sitework and Foundation

Sitework sets the stage for everything downstream. Common surprises: rock, groundwater, buried debris, and utility conflicts. The geotechnical report is your insurance policy — pay for the borings.

### Phase 3 — Structure

Most limited-service and select-service hotels today use **wood-frame or cold-formed steel** over a concrete podium. Full-service and high-rise properties use cast-in-place concrete or structural steel. Structure selection drives cost, schedule, and long-term acoustic performance in the guestrooms — acoustic modeling should be part of the design package, not an afterthought.

### Phase 4 — MEP Systems

Hotel MEP is denser than typical office or retail. Each guestroom is essentially a small apartment with its own bathroom — multiply that by 100–300 rooms and the rough-in complexity is substantial. PTAC units, water heater systems (centralized vs. distributed), and fire suppression coordination are the three most schedule-critical MEP scopes.

### Phase 5 — Interior Finishes and FF&E

Hospitality finishes are high-tolerance work. Tile grout lines in bathrooms, millwork tolerances at case goods, and paint sheen specifications are all items the brand QA team will inspect at the pre-opening walk. Use a finish specification matrix tied to the brand PIP from the start of design — don't let the finish schedule drift.

### Phase 6 — Commissioning, Punch, and Opening

The final 8–12 weeks of a hotel project are the highest-stress period. Life safety systems (fire alarm, sprinkler, emergency lighting) require third-party commissioning and AHJ sign-off before occupancy. Plan for a minimum of two brand walks and a final punch list before the certificate of occupancy is issued.

## FAQ

### How much does it cost to build a 100-room hotel?

A 100-room limited-service hotel in a mid-tier U.S. market costs roughly **$16M–$25M in hard costs**, depending on land, local labor market, and brand standards. Add 18–25% for soft costs, land, and pre-opening, and total development cost commonly reaches **$20M–$32M** for a prototypical limited-service project. Full-service or urban projects run materially higher.

### How long does it take to build a hotel from scratch?

From initial site control to opening day, most hotel projects take **24–48 months**. A clean, entitled site with a prototypical brand design can shorten this to 18–24 months. Complex entitlements, custom design, or mixed-use programs routinely push past 36 months.

### Do I need a general contractor who specializes in hotels?

Yes. Hospitality projects have brand-mandated specifications, a fixed soft-opening date tied to marketing and franchise agreements, and complex life safety requirements. A GC without hotel experience will miss brand PIP requirements and struggle to coordinate the FF&E installation sequence with construction closeout. The cost of learning that lesson mid-project is high.

### What is a hotel PIP and why does it matter in construction?

A **PIP (property improvement plan)** is the brand's specification document defining required finishes, furniture standards, and systems for a property to earn or maintain flag certification. PIPs must be incorporated into the construction documents before permit submission — retrofitting PIP requirements after construction begins is expensive and delays opening.

### What permits are required to build a hotel?

At minimum: a **building permit** covering structural, architectural, and life safety; separate **MEP permits** (mechanical, electrical, plumbing); a **fire suppression permit**; and a **health department permit** for any food service or pool. High-rise, historic district, or coastal properties trigger additional state or federal review layers.
