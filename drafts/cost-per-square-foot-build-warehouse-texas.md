## What Does It Cost Per Square Foot to Build a Warehouse in Texas?

The short answer: **$40–$120/sqft for the building shell**, plus $15–$30/sqft for site work, utilities, and soft costs. Most standard distribution warehouses in Texas secondary markets land between $55–$80/sqft all-in; first-ring suburban DFW or Austin projects run $80–$110/sqft.

### Texas Warehouse Cost by Construction Type

| Construction Type | Shell Cost ($/sqft) | Best For |

| Conventional steel frame + masonry | $75–$120 | Heavy manufacturing, cold storage, high-clear |
| Cold storage / refrigerated | $100–$160+ | Food, pharma, temperature-sensitive goods |

## What Drives Warehouse Construction Cost Per Square Foot in Texas?

**Clear height is the single biggest cost lever.** A 24-foot clear warehouse costs materially less than a 36-foot or 40-foot clear facility. Each additional foot of clear height adds structural steel, bracing, and often a heavier foundation.

### Clear Height vs. Cost Impact

| Clear Height | Relative Cost Impact | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| 18–22 ft | Base | Light storage, single-user |
| 24 ft | +5–8% | Standard distribution |
| 28–32 ft | +12–18% | Modern logistics / multi-level racking |
| 36–40 ft | +20–30% | High-cube e-commerce, automated racking |

**Other major cost drivers:**

- **Dock doors and grade-level doors:** Budget $8,000–$18,000 per dock door (leveler, seal, light) and $4,000–$8,000 per grade-level door.
- **Office and mezzanine build-out:** Finished office space adds $80–$120/sqft over the raw warehouse rate.
- **Fire suppression:** ESFR sprinkler systems add $4–$7/sqft and are required at most clear-height thresholds under Texas Fire Code.
- **Electrical service:** Heavy manufacturing or EV charging infrastructure can add $10–$20/sqft over standard distribution power.
- **Site work and grading:** Texas clay and caliche soils can require significant over-excavation and engineered fill. Budget $8–$20/sqft depending on site conditions — [VERIFY with Maxx Builders site work actuals].

## What Are the Soft Costs for a Texas Warehouse Project?

Soft costs typically run **10–15% of hard construction costs** for a straightforward warehouse and can reach 20% for complex industrial projects.

| Soft Cost Item | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Architectural & engineering fees | 3–6% of construction cost |
| Permitting and impact fees | $0.50–$3.00/sqft (varies by jurisdiction) |
| Geotech and environmental | $15,000–$60,000 depending on site |
| Construction management / GC fee | 8–15% of total construction cost |
| Financing carry during construction | Varies with rate environment |
| FF&E (dock equipment, lighting) | $2–$5/sqft |

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does it take to build a warehouse in Texas?**
A pre-engineered metal building of 20,000–50,000 sqft typically takes 6–10 months from contract to certificate of occupancy in Texas. Tilt-up concrete projects of similar size run 9–14 months. Timeline varies significantly with permitting jurisdiction and design complexity. [VERIFY against Maxx Builders average project timeline data.]

**What is the minimum viable size for a warehouse in Texas?**
Most commercial warehouse projects start at 5,000 sqft. Below that threshold, per-square-foot costs rise sharply because fixed infrastructure costs (site work, utilities, sprinklers, dock doors) spread over fewer square feet.

**Do I need a general contractor or can I owner-build a warehouse in Texas?**
Texas does not prohibit owner-building for commercial projects, but most lenders, insurers, and jurisdictions require a licensed General Contractor for projects over a certain value. For any warehouse exceeding $500,000 in construction cost, a licensed GC is strongly advised. [VERIFY: confirm Texas licensing threshold from TDLR.]

**Are there tax incentives for building a warehouse in Texas?**
Texas offers several industrial incentives including Chapter 380/381 economic development agreements, property tax abatements through individual county/city programs, and enterprise zone designations in qualifying areas. The specific incentive available depends on location, job creation commitments, and capital investment thresholds. [VERIFY: consult a Texas economic development attorney before citing specific programs.]

**What is the difference between a tilt-up and pre-engineered metal building for warehousing?**
Tilt-up concrete panel construction uses cast-in-place concrete wall panels tilted into position on site. It offers better long-term durability, fire resistance, and appearance for multi-tenant or class-A facilities. Pre-engineered metal buildings (PEMB) are faster to erect and lower in initial cost, making them the dominant choice for owner-operated distribution and manufacturing in Texas secondary markets.
