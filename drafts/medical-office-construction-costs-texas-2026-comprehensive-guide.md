## What Drives Cost in Texas Medical Office Construction?

Medical construction costs more than standard office because of higher system density and regulatory requirements.

**MEP (Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing)** is the biggest swing factor. Medical facilities require medical-grade HVAC with specific air-exchange rates per ASHRAE 170, often separate air-handling units per zone, and in some cases negative-pressure rooms. In a typical medical office build, MEP runs 30–40% of total hard costs vs. 15–20% in standard commercial office.

**Key cost drivers:**

- **Plumbing density** — clinical sinks at every exam room, scrub stations, medical gas rough-in
- **Structural** — shielded rooms (X-ray, fluoroscopy) require lead lining or concrete mass
- **Accessibility** — Texas requires TAS (Texas Accessibility Standards) compliance, which is stricter than federal ADA on exam room clearances
- **Regulatory review** — TDLR reviews accessibility plans for all new commercial buildings in Texas; ambulatory surgery centers trigger additional DSHS licensing and state plan review requirements on top of the standard city building permit
- **Fire and life safety** — NFPA 101 healthcare occupancy classifications

## How Much Do Medical Gas and Radiation Shielding Add to Construction Cost?

Medical gas systems and radiation shielding are two line items that routinely surprise owners in early budgets — they appear as single entries but can add $15–$65+ per square foot in the clinical areas they touch. How much depends on gas outlet density and which imaging modalities the practice runs.

### Medical Gas Systems

Medical gas systems are governed by NFPA 99 (Health Care Facilities Code) and must be installed and commissioned by certified medical gas contractors. The system runs from a manifold room through zone valves to individual outlets at the point of care, with master alarm panels for pressure monitoring throughout.

**Gas types required by practice type:**

| Gas | Typical Use | Required In |
|---|---|---|
| Oxygen (O2) | Clinical care, procedure support | Virtually all clinical spaces |
| Vacuum | Suction at exam and procedure rooms | Anywhere procedures are performed |
| Medical air | Respiratory therapy, equipment | Procedure and surgical areas |
| Nitrous oxide | Dental, some procedure suites | Must be paired with WAGD |
| WAGD | Waste anesthetic gas disposal | All spaces where inhalation anesthetics are used |
| CO2 | Insufflation for endoscopy | Endoscopy suites only |

**Per-outlet installed cost** (includes rough-in piping, outlet head, zone valve allocation, alarm panel share, and commissioning):

| Outlet Type | Installed Cost Range |
|---|---|
| Oxygen | $800–$1,500 |
| Vacuum | $700–$1,200 |
| Medical air | $900–$1,400 |
| Nitrous oxide | $1,000–$1,800 |
| Full group (O2 + vacuum + medical air) | $2,200–$4,000 |

**Area cost impact across clinical zones:**

- **Primary care, sparse gas (O2 and vacuum in select rooms):** $10–$18/sq ft in clinical areas
- **Multi-specialty, moderate gas density:** $18–$28/sq ft
- **Procedure-heavy or surgical suite (full gas train with WAGD):** $30–$45/sq ft for medical gas scope alone

Also budget separately for zone valve boxes ($2,500–$5,000 per zone), master alarm panel ($8,000–$20,000 depending on system size), and NFPA 99 third-party commissioning certification ($3,000–$8,000). Health departments and lenders typically require the certification before occupancy is granted.

### Radiation Shielding

Shielding is designed by a qualified medical physicist who calculates required lead equivalency based on workload, radiation energy, adjacent space occupancy, and applicable regulatory limits. The GC then implements the physicist's specification — shielding and structural scope are interdependent and must be coordinated before construction documents are finalized.

**Shielding cost by modality:**

| Modality | Primary Material | Lead Equivalency | Shielding Cost (room perimeter, per sq ft) |
|---|---|---|---|
| General diagnostic X-ray | Lead-lined drywall | 1.0–1.5 mm Pb | $18–$35 |
| Fluoroscopy suite | Lead-lined drywall + lead batt | 1.5–2.5 mm Pb | $30–$50 |
| CT scanner | Dense concrete or CMU | 4–8 in. concrete equivalent | $35–$65 |
| MRI (RF cage) | Copper or aluminum sheeting | N/A — electromagnetic isolation, not radiation | $50–$120 |
| Nuclear medicine | Lead + reinforced concrete | 2.5–5.0+ mm Pb | $60–$120 |
| Linear accelerator vault | Poured concrete bunker | 5–10 ft thick walls | $500–$2,000+ per sq ft of vault area |

**Structural implications to budget before design begins:**

- **CT:** Gantry and table combined weigh 2,000–5,000 lbs. Slab thickening or a grade beam under the equipment footprint is common. Confirm with the structural engineer before construction documents are complete.
- **MRI:** Magnet weight ranges from 5,000 lbs (1.5T) to 25,000 lbs (3T). Structural engineer involvement is mandatory. The quench pipe (cryogen pressure-relief path) must penetrate the roof — locate it before the structural frame is designed. Ferrous materials must be excluded from a radius around the magnet defined by the physicist.
- **Linac vault:** Reinforced concrete walls commonly run 5–8 ft thick. The vault is poured before building interior finishes begin. Shielding design must be complete before sitework starts.

**Total shielded room cost premium** (above standard room construction, inclusive of shielding materials, structural prep, and physicist fees):

| Room Type | Cost Premium Over Standard Room |
|---|---|
| General X-ray room (250–400 sq ft) | +$40,000–$80,000 |
| CT suite | +$80,000–$150,000 |
| MRI suite | +$100,000–$300,000+ |
| Linac vault | +$500,000–$2,000,000 (shielding and structural only) |

## How to Build a Medical Office Around an Operating Practice

Running construction in or adjacent to an active medical practice is one of the most complex tenant improvement scenarios — and one of the most consistently underestimated. Construction generates dust, noise, vibration, and pressure differentials that can harm immunocompromised patients and disrupt sensitive equipment. ICRA (Infection Control Risk Assessment) is the framework that governs how that risk gets managed, and it directly shapes how you can phase and schedule the work.

### What Is ICRA?

ICRA is a pre-construction process in which the GC, owner, and clinical lead jointly classify the risk level of the proposed scope and agree on physical control measures before work begins. The Joint Commission requires it for accredited facilities. For non-accredited ambulatory practices, it is best practice and increasingly expected by health system affiliates.

Work is classified into four classes based on activity type and proximity to patient care:

| ICRA Class | Typical Work | Minimum Control Measures |
|---|---|---|
| Class I | Minor repairs, non-invasive surface work | Dust mask, seal work area, wipe surfaces |
| Class II | Small-scale short-duration demo, limited wall openings | Solid barrier, avoid patient-care hours if adjacent, HEPA vacuum |
| Class III | Moderate-to-large demo, new construction adjacent to occupied clinical areas | Hard-wall containment, negative pressure in work zone, HEPA air filtration, dedicated debris route |
| Class IV | Major demo or construction immediately adjacent to high-risk patient-care areas | Sealed anteroom, continuous negative pressure with air monitoring, daily cleanup protocol |

Most occupied medical office TI projects fall into Class III. If the practice treats immunocompromised patients, operates adjacent to an ASC, or shares an air handler with a procedure suite, classify as IV.

### Physical Separation Requirements for Class III and IV

**Hard-wall barrier:** Floor-to-deck drywall partition with taped and sealed penetrations. Poly sheeting is not acceptable for Class III or IV. Installed cost: **$4–$8/sq ft of barrier perimeter.** Budget for demolition and patching on completion as well.

**Negative pressure:** The construction zone must run at lower air pressure than adjacent clinical areas — air flows *toward* the construction zone, not away from it. Requires blocking the zone's return air and running HEPA air filtration units exhausting to the exterior. Rental cost: **$300–$600/unit/week.** A 2,000 sq ft zone typically requires two to four units running continuously throughout the project.

**Sticky walk-off mats:** At all barrier entry and exit points to capture particulate from workers' footwear. Replaced daily.

**Dedicated debris removal path:** All construction waste routes away from clinical corridors and waiting areas. May require a temporary exterior access opening or scheduling all debris hauls during off-hours.

**Daily air monitoring:** Pressure differential and particulate levels checked at each shift change. If the zone goes positive pressure relative to clinical space, work stops until isolation is restored and documented.

### Noise and Vibration Windows

Core drilling, concrete cutting, pneumatic demolition, and hammer-intensive framing cannot run during active clinical hours. This constraint is the biggest single driver of cost premium in occupied medical TI work.

Texas municipalities generally allow construction 7AM–7PM. Medical practices typically operate 8AM–5PM. That leaves a one-hour morning buffer and a two-hour evening buffer for moderate-noise work on weekdays — not a workable window for slab demolition or structural drilling.

**Off-hours labor premiums:**

| Shift | Premium Over Standard Daytime Rate |
|---|---|
| Evening (after 6PM on weekdays) | 15–20% |
| Night (after 10PM) | 20–30% |
| Weekend daytime | 20–25% |
| Weekend night | 30–40% |

A scope that would take four weeks on a clear unoccupied site can take eight to ten weeks when loud work is limited to a four-hour window on weekday evenings plus weekends.

**Vibration-sensitive equipment:** MRI magnets, surgical microscopes, ophthalmology equipment, and precision lab instruments have vibration tolerances that may be exceeded by slab work in adjacent spaces. If the construction zone is adjacent to any of these, a pre-construction vibration baseline study ($2,000–$5,000) is warranted, and certain work phases may need to be scheduled around equipment downtime.

### Phasing Strategies

**Sequential phasing:** Complete one zone or wing fully before beginning the next. Simplest ICRA management — one barrier, one work zone at a time. Total schedule is the longest of the three approaches, but operational disruption at any given time is contained.

**Parallel phasing with swing space:** Temporarily relocate specific clinical functions from the active construction zone to a swing space — a sublet suite nearby or a modular clinical unit — while that zone is built out, then return to the completed space. Texas temporary medical office sublease runs **$20–$45/sq ft/year.** A 3,000 sq ft swing space for six months costs $30,000–$67,500 in lease cost alone, before any fit-out or relocation expense. The trade-off: the practice operates closer to full capacity throughout.

**Off-hours build-out:** For smaller scopes with a short disruption window, run all construction outside clinical hours. No swing space, no phased access — all work happens nights and weekends. Labor premium is highest, but the practice has no patient-care-hour disruption.

### Cost Premium for Occupied Medical TI

A practice-adjacent TI project should budget **20–35% more** than an equivalent single-phase unoccupied build:

| Factor | Estimated Additional Cost |
|---|---|
| Hard-wall barrier construction and removal | $4–$8/sq ft of barrier perimeter |
| HEPA filtration rental (Class III, 90-day project) | $8,000–$20,000 per zone |
| Off-hours labor premium | 15–35% of affected scope labor |
| Sequential re-mobilizations | $5,000–$15,000 per additional mobilization |
| Swing space lease (6-month TI) | $30,000–$70,000 depending on market and size |
| Extended schedule vs. single-phase TI | 20–40% longer overall timeline |

A $750,000 TI scoped as a single unoccupied phase should be re-estimated at $900,000–$1,050,000 for a properly phased Class III ICRA-compliant occupied delivery. That premium reflects patient safety compliance, not inefficiency — and it is far less than the cost of a Joint Commission citation or a reportable infection-control event.

### What to Confirm With Your GC Before Contracting

1. **Require an ICRA pre-construction meeting** with your clinical lead and the GC before work begins. A GC unfamiliar with this process is a signal worth investigating.
2. **Get a written noise schedule** — which tasks require off-hours, what the daily plan looks like, and the escalation process if a loud task runs over.
3. **Confirm barrier specification** — hard-wall for Class III and IV, not poly sheeting.
4. **Walk the debris removal route** before work starts and confirm it avoids all patient-facing areas.
5. **Assign air monitoring responsibility** — who checks pressure differential daily, what the threshold is for stopping work, and how it is documented.

## How to Finance Medical Office Construction in Texas

Construction financing for medical office follows a two-phase structure in most cases: a short-term construction loan that converts to permanent financing at certificate of occupancy. Understanding the loan types available — and what lenders require before they will commit — is critical to aligning your budget timeline with your construction timeline.

### Construction-to-Permanent (C-to-P) Loans

C-to-P loans are the standard vehicle for ground-up medical office construction. The lender funds construction draws as work progresses, then the loan automatically converts to a permanent mortgage at CO without requiring a separate closing. Typical terms in the 2026 Texas market:

- **LTV:** 75–80% of completed appraised value (not cost)
- **Construction period:** 12–18 months, interest-only on drawn balance
- **Permanent term:** 20–25 years amortization
- **Rate:** Variable during construction (prime-based), fixed or floating at conversion depending on lender

The construction appraisal is a key underwriting document — the lender orders it based on the plans and specifications, not a finished building. Complete construction documents and a signed AIA contract make the appraiser's job easier and improve your chances of a valuation that supports your requested loan amount.

### SBA 7(a)

The SBA 7(a) program works well for medical practices with total project costs under $5 million. Physician ownership of the practice makes this particularly attractive — lenders look favorably on medical practices because of stable, recurring revenue and historically low default rates.

- **Maximum loan:** $5 million
- **LTV:** Up to 90% in some cases, with SBA guarantee reducing lender exposure
- **Term:** Up to 25 years for real estate
- **Use:** Land acquisition, construction, equipment, working capital — all in one loan

The SBA 7(a) requires personal guarantees from owners with 20%+ equity stake. Processing times run 45–90 days from complete application to approval for most Texas SBA lenders.

### SBA 504

For owner-occupied medical facilities over $2 million in total project cost, the SBA 504 program is often the most cost-effective financing structure available. The 504 splits financing into three tranches:

- **Senior lender:** approximately 50% of project cost, conventional terms
- **CDC (Certified Development Company):** approximately 40% of project cost, 25-year term at a below-market fixed rate set at the time of debenture sale
- **Borrower equity:** 10% down (vs. 20–30% on conventional commercial real estate)

The fixed rate on the CDC tranche is the program's most compelling feature — it eliminates long-term rate risk on a facility you plan to occupy for decades. The trade-off is processing time: SBA 504 closings typically take 60–90 days and require a dedicated CDC partner to originate and service the CDC tranche.

On a recent 4,500 sq ft multi-specialty clinic project in the Houston suburbs, Maxx Builders helped the physician group structure an SBA 504 loan covering 40% of project cost at a fixed rate, with C-to-P construction financing from a Texas regional lender for the balance. Pre-construction estimating services allowed the lender to issue a commitment letter before construction documents were complete, compressing the overall pre-construction timeline by six weeks.

### USDA Community Facilities

For medical practices in rural Texas markets — generally defined as communities under 20,000 population — the USDA Community Facilities program offers below-market fixed-rate direct loans and loan guarantees for healthcare facility construction. This program is underutilized in Texas. If your project is in a rural county, contact your local USDA Rural Development office before defaulting to a conventional construction loan.

### What Lenders Require at Pre-Construction

Before issuing a construction loan commitment, most Texas lenders will require:

- Stamped construction drawings (not necessarily 100% CDs, but beyond schematic)
- GC license verification and current certificate of insurance
- GC performance and payment bond commitment
- Project cost breakdown by CSI division or equivalent
- Signed AIA A101 (or equivalent) Owner-Contractor Agreement
- Evidence of site control (deed or executed ground lease)

The earlier you engage a GC with pre-construction estimating services, the earlier you can satisfy lender documentation requirements — and the earlier you can lock financing terms.

| Loan Type | Typical LTV | Term | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| C-to-P conventional | 75–80% | 20–25 yr perm | All market types, fastest closing |
| SBA 7(a) | Up to 90% | Up to 25 yr | Projects under $5M, physician-owned practices |
| SBA 504 | 90% (10% down) | 25 yr fixed (CDC tranche) | Owner-occupied, $2M+ projects, long hold |

## Texas Regulatory Approvals: TDLR, DSHS, and What Triggers State Review

Texas has two primary state-level review bodies that healthcare construction teams need to understand before design begins. Conflating them — or not knowing which applies — leads to permit delays, scope revisions, and in some cases, facilities that cannot legally open.

### TDLR: Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation

TDLR reviews accessibility plans for any new or renovated commercial building in Texas under the Texas Accessibility Standards (TAS). This is mandatory for all new construction and significant renovations of commercial buildings, including medical offices, clinics, and dental offices — not optional, not project-size dependent.

TDLR plan review runs parallel to city building permit review, not sequentially after it. Submitting to TDLR only after city permit approval is a common scheduling mistake that adds weeks to your timeline.

**TDLR plan review timelines (2026):**

- Standard review: 10–15 business days
- Expedited review: 5 business days (additional fee of $500–$2,500 depending on project value)

TAS has requirements that exceed federal ADA minimums and are consistently missed by GCs without Texas-specific experience — exam room clear floor space, accessible route widths in clinical corridors, and accessible parking ratios for medical land uses are the most common gaps found in plan review.

### DSHS: Texas Department of State Health Services

DSHS facility licensing applies when a facility meets the definition of a Licensed Health Care Facility under Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 241. General acute care hospitals and ambulatory surgical centers are the primary DSHS-licensed facility types relevant to outpatient construction.

Standard physician offices and clinics below the surgical threshold are **not** DSHS-licensed facilities. They operate under TDI (Texas Department of Insurance) for liability purposes, but DSHS does not review or license them as healthcare facilities.

### What Triggers ASC Classification

An ambulatory surgical center classification — and the DSHS licensing and state plan review that comes with it — is triggered when the facility performs procedures requiring anesthesia beyond local (topical or infiltration). If your practice will use moderate sedation, deep sedation, or general anesthesia for any procedure, it is an ASC under Texas law, and the building must be designed, reviewed, and licensed accordingly.

GCs need to know this before permit submission, because ASC HVAC requirements, room sizing, and recovery area requirements are substantially different from a standard medical office shell. Discovering the ASC classification requirement after construction documents are complete means redesign, restamping, and restarting the permit review process from the beginning.

### Local Health Department Overlay

Some Texas counties have additional review requirements that layer on top of state review:

- **Dental offices:** Harris County, Dallas County, and Tarrant County health departments require separate permits for dental water lines, nitrous oxide systems, and WAGD installations. Confirm with the local health authority before finalizing MEP drawings.
- **Ambulatory surgery centers:** DSHS on-site survey is required before an ASC can receive its license and begin operating. Budget 4–8 weeks after CO for this process, and do not schedule patient procedures before it is complete.

### Summary: Which Review Body Applies?

| Facility Type | TDLR | DSHS | Local Health Dept |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard physician office / clinic | Yes | No | Generally no |
| Dental office | Yes | No | Varies by county |
| Ambulatory surgery center (ASC) | Yes | Yes | Yes (DSHS-coordinated) |
| General acute care hospital | Yes | Yes | Yes |

## Build vs. Lease vs. Buy Existing: Which Makes More Financial Sense for a Texas Medical Practice?

The correct answer depends on your time horizon, your need for customization, and where local lease rates sit relative to ownership costs. For practices with a 10-year or longer commitment to a market, the economics of ownership are compelling in most Texas metros. Here is how the three options compare for a 3,000 sq ft primary care practice in DFW.

### Option 1: Build Ground-Up

- **Construction cost:** $600,000–$900,000 ($200–$300/sq ft on owned structure)
- **Land:** $300,000–$600,000 for a 0.5–0.75-acre suburban DFW parcel (2026)
- **Total all-in:** $900,000–$1,500,000
- **Lease obligations:** None
- **Equity at Year 10:** Full ownership of improved real property; current DFW medical office cap rates suggest a well-located 3,000 sq ft practice building is worth $800,000–$1,200,000 at Year 10

Ground-up gives you complete design control — every exam room, every utility stub-out, every HVAC zone is yours to specify. The tradeoff is the longest timeline (18–24 months to first patient) and the highest upfront capital requirement.

### Option 2: Lease MOB Space

- **Annual rent:** $28–$40/sq ft NNN in DFW (2026 market)
- **Typical escalation:** 3% annually
- **10-year NNN cumulative rent:** approximately $980,000–$1,400,000 cumulative, before annual escalations compound
- **Equity at Year 10:** Zero
- **TI allowance:** $40–$80/sq ft from landlord in current DFW market — covers basic finish-out, not specialty medical build-out

Leasing minimizes upfront capital and gets you to first patient in 4–9 months. For early-stage practices or those entering a new market, it is the right call. For an established practice with stable patient volume, 10 years of NNN rent represents a down payment on a building you will never own.

### Option 3: Buy and Renovate Existing Medical Office

- **Purchase price:** $150–$250/sq ft for Class B medical office in DFW (2026)
- **Renovation cost:** $60–$120/sq ft to update finishes, MEP, and accessibility compliance
- **Total all-in:** $630,000–$1,100,000 for 3,000 sq ft
- **Timeline:** 12–18 months total (acquisition + renovation)
- **Equity at Year 10:** Full ownership; renovation typically resets building value above purchase price

Buying existing is the middle path — faster than ground-up, builds equity unlike leasing, and often available at a lower all-in cost than new construction when existing MEP infrastructure is serviceable.

### 10-Year Comparison Table (3,000 sq ft Primary Care, DFW)

| Option | 10-Year Total Cost | Equity at Year 10 | Flexibility Rating |
|---|---|---|---|
| Build ground-up | $1.2M–$1.8M (all-in) | High — owned improved real property | Low — committed to location and layout |
| Lease NNN | $980K–$1.4M (rent only) | None | High — can relocate at lease expiration |
| Buy and renovate | $630K–$1.1M (all-in) | Moderate-to-high | Low-to-moderate — limited by existing structure |

For practices with a 10-year or longer horizon and stable anchor patient volume, ground-up or buy-and-renovate beats leasing in most Texas markets when you account for equity accumulation and the elimination of rent escalation exposure. Leasing wins when the practice is early-stage, entering a new market, or needs to open quickly without large capital deployment. From Maxx Builders project experience and current market conditions: the break-even point in DFW typically falls between years six and eight for a well-located owner-occupied building — after which every dollar of rent paid is a direct drag against a net worth position you could have been building.

## What to Look for in a Healthcare General Contractor in Texas

Not every commercial GC is qualified to build medical space. The gap between a commercial office build and a medical office build — ICRA protocols, medical gas certification requirements, shielding coordination, TDLR accessibility review — is large enough that using an underqualified GC creates real risk to your budget, your schedule, and your ability to open.

Here are five qualifications that differentiate a healthcare GC from a general commercial contractor:

### 1. Demonstrated ICRA Experience

Ask for a project list of occupied medical TI work with the ICRA classification used on each project. A GC who has never managed a Class III ICRA containment on an active clinical floor is a training exercise at your expense. You want to see multiple completed projects with ICRA compliance documentation — not just familiarity with the term.

### 2. In-House or Dedicated Medical Gas Subcontractor

NFPA 99 requires medical gas systems to be installed and commissioned by personnel with ASSE 6010 (installation) and ASSE 6030 (inspection and testing) certifications. Ask the GC which medical gas subcontractor they use, confirm that subcontractor's ASSE certification is current, and ask when that sub was last on a comparable project in your market. A GC without a standing relationship with a certified medical gas sub will be scrambling to find one during bidding.

### 3. Experience Coordinating with a Medical Physicist

Radiation shielding scope cannot be fully defined until a medical physicist has calculated lead equivalency requirements. A GC who has never managed this coordination will either omit physicist fees from the budget, fail to sequence the shielding drawings into the permit set correctly, or both. Ask for a specific project example where the GC coordinated physicist-specified shielding scope through design, permitting, and construction.

### 4. Track Record with TDLR and Texas Health Department Processes

TDLR plan review, ASC licensing surveys, and county-level dental or WAGD permits are Texas-specific processes. A GC who primarily works in other states — or who has only built standard commercial office in Texas — may not know that TDLR review runs parallel to city permits, or that some counties require separate plumbing permits for dental gas systems. Ask how many Texas medical office permits they have pulled in the last three years and in which jurisdictions.

## 2026 Texas Medical Construction Cost Outlook: What's Affecting Prices Now

Medical office construction costs in Texas moderated slightly from the 2023–2024 peak, but they are not returning to pre-2020 levels. Here is what is driving costs right now and what owners should lock in early to protect their budgets.

### Steel and Aluminum

Structural steel remains 15–20% above 2019 baseline prices as of mid-2026. The tariff cycle that drove the 2022–2023 spike has stabilized, but the price reset is not coming. For medical office buildings with structural steel framing — typical for multi-story MOBs and any building with a heavy imaging load — budget steel at current levels and do not plan on price relief before your project breaks ground.

### Copper

Medical-dense buildings consume 3–5x the copper per square foot of standard commercial office — medical gas tubing, high-density electrical service, and data infrastructure all run on copper. Current copper price volatility directly affects your MEP budget in ways that a standard commercial GC may not flag proactively. On a 5,000 sq ft multi-specialty clinic, copper content in the MEP scope can represent $35,000–$65,000 in material cost alone. Get a copper-price-current MEP estimate — ask when the MEP subcontractor last updated their copper pricing in the budget.

### Medical-Grade AHU Lead Times

Medical-grade air handling units from major manufacturers are running 16–28 weeks from order to delivery in 2026, compared to 8–12 weeks pre-2022. This is the single most schedule-critical long-lead item in a medical office build. If you wait until the general contractor is mobilized on-site to order the AHUs, you will sit idle on a framed building waiting for mechanical equipment.

Best practice: identify the AHU specification during design development, get submittals approved, and issue a purchase order for long-lead equipment before the building permit is issued. This is standard procedure for experienced healthcare GCs and a consistent gap for GCs who primarily build standard commercial office.

### HVAC Labor: TAB Technicians

Certified TAB (Test and Balance) technicians are the tightest skilled trade in the Texas healthcare construction market in 2026. TAB work cannot be performed until HVAC installation is substantially complete, and a TAB report is required by the building official — and often by the health department — before occupancy is granted. If your GC's HVAC subcontractor does not have a committed TAB contractor on their project list before construction starts, your commissioning phase will be delayed.

### Cost Trend Summary

Hard costs for Texas medical office construction are holding in the $200–$450/sq ft range through 2026, with primary cost pressure coming from MEP labor rates, long-lead equipment, and copper. The era of 20%+ annual escalation from 2021–2023 has passed, but a return to pre-pandemic pricing is not on the horizon. Budget assumptions built on pre-2020 cost data will underfund your project. From Maxx Builders project experience and current market conditions: the right planning assumption for 2026 is flat-to-modest escalation, with the biggest schedule and budget risk sitting in MEP equipment lead times, not labor or structural costs.

### Pre-Construction Lockdown Best Practices

To protect your budget against the current cost environment:

1. **Lock structural steel** in the pre-construction phase, before permit is issued. Steel mill lead times and price volatility make late procurement a budget risk.
2. **Issue purchase orders for long-lead HVAC equipment** at permit submittal, not permit issuance. The 16–28 week clock starts when the order is placed.
3. **Get a copper-current MEP estimate** — ask your GC when the MEP subcontractor last updated their copper pricing.
4. **Confirm TAB contractor availability** before executing the HVAC subcontract.

## FAQ

### How much does it cost to build a 5,000 sq ft medical office in Texas?

A 5,000 sq ft medical office in Texas runs $1.0M–$2.25M in hard construction costs in 2026, depending on specialty type and location. Add 20–30% for soft costs (design, permitting, FF&E, technology) to estimate total project cost.

### Is it cheaper to build or lease medical office space in Texas?

Leasing a shell and doing a tenant improvement is almost always cheaper in year one — $80–$200/sq ft vs. $200–$450/sq ft for ground-up. Build-to-own makes sense when you need a highly customized facility, want long-term cost control, or the local lease market is tight. For practices with a 10-year or longer horizon in a stable Texas market, the 10-year TCO of ownership consistently beats leasing when you account for equity accumulation and rent escalation. The break-even point in DFW typically falls between years six and eight for a well-located owner-occupied building.

### How long does medical office construction take in Texas?

Ground-up: 12–24 months from design kickoff to certificate of occupancy. Tenant improvements: 4–9 months depending on scope and local permitting timelines.

### What is the most expensive part of building a medical office?

MEP systems (HVAC, plumbing, medical gas, electrical) typically represent 30–40% of total hard costs in a medical office build — more in procedure-heavy facilities. This is the primary reason medical construction costs 2–3x standard commercial office.

### Do I need a healthcare-specialized general contractor in Texas?

Not legally required, but strongly advisable. Medical facilities have specific HVAC, plumbing, and infection-control requirements that general commercial contractors consistently underestimate. Cost overruns from MEP scope gaps and re-work are common when contractors lack healthcare experience.
