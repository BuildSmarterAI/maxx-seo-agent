## Answer-First Introduction

Medical office construction in Texas costs roughly $200 to $450 per square foot for new build-out in 2026, with total project budgets for a 5,000–20,000 sq ft clinic ranging from $1 million to over $9 million once land, soft costs, and equipment are included. Costs vary sharply by market, specialty type, and finish level — a primary care suite in a suburban Tier 2 Texas city runs considerably less than an oncology or imaging center in central Houston or Austin.

## What Drives Medical Office Construction Costs in Texas?

**The short answer:** Medical construction costs are driven by four factors — structural/MEP complexity, market labor rates, regulatory requirements (HHSC, FGI Guidelines), and the finish level of clinical vs. administrative space.

## Texas Regulatory Requirements That Add Cost

**The short answer:** Texas medical facilities must comply with HHSC licensing rules, FGI Guidelines, and local jurisdiction codes — each layer adds scope and schedule.

### HHSC and FGI Guidelines

The Texas Health and Human Services Commission (HHSC) adopts the **Facility Guidelines Institute (FGI) Guidelines for Design and Construction of Outpatient Facilities**. For any licensed healthcare facility, the FGI minimum room sizes, ventilation rates, and handwashing station requirements are mandatory — not suggestions. This adds roughly 8–12% to MEP costs relative to a standard commercial office.

Key compliance items that catch first-time medical developers off guard:

- **Air change rates:** Procedure rooms require 15+ ACH; most offices run 6.
- **Exhaust separation:** Hazardous exhaust (lab, soiled utility) cannot recirculate.
- **ADA plus HHSC clearances:** Exam room maneuvering clearances exceed base ADA minimums.
- **Hand-wash station density:** HHSC specifies stations per room type, not just per floor.

## New Build vs. Tenant Improvement (TI) vs. Conversion: What Pencils in Texas?

**The short answer:** Tenant improvement of existing Class B/C office is often 30–40% cheaper than ground-up if the shell MEP capacity is adequate — but inadequate HVAC or electrical capacity erases that advantage fast.

### Decision Framework

1. **Existing shell MEP capacity** — medical HVAC and electrical loads are 3–5x a standard office. If the existing building can't handle it, TI cost approaches new build.
2. **Ceiling height** — 10 ft minimum clearance recommended; 9 ft shells add coordination cost.
3. **Structural loading** — imaging equipment (MRI: 10,000–30,000 lbs) may require slab reinforcement regardless of TI vs. new build.
4. **Zoning / entitlement risk** — medical use is typically permitted in commercial zones but ASCs require conditional use permits in many Texas jurisdictions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does it take to build a medical office in Texas?

A standard tenant improvement for a 3,000–5,000 sq ft primary care suite in Texas typically takes **4–7 months** from permit submission to certificate of occupancy. Ground-up medical office buildings run **12–20 months** depending on size, market, and HHSC licensing requirements. Shell delivery and TI are often sequenced, so total time from land contract to opening day can exceed 24 months for a new-build owner-occupied campus.

### What is the biggest cost mistake in medical office construction?

Underestimating MEP scope is the most common cost error. Medical clients routinely budget commercial-office MEP rates and are surprised when the HVAC system alone costs two to three times more per square foot than a standard office. The second most common error is failing to account for commissioning and HHSC inspection time in the schedule.

### Are construction costs in Texas lower than national averages?

Generally yes — Texas labor rates are below those in California, New York, and the Pacific Northwest by 20–35% for comparable scopes. However, material costs are similar nationally, and the Texas market's construction volume has compressed that labor advantage in DFW and Austin since 2021.
